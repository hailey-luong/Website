Defining the Scope

Target Users

Although we aimed to develop a product that could be used by children to learn CPR, we recognized that other groups of people would be involved as well. We outlined these as:

Primary users

Children aged 10-13 who do not know First Aid/CPR

Secondary users

Trainers, instructors, teachers who teach CPR to children

Tertiary users

Parents who enroll their children in First Aid/CPR workshops

CPR Process

To better understand the scope of the project, it was necessary to learn the process of CPR. If the casualty is either unconscious or has consented to receive CPR if conscious, these are the basic steps to follow. The last two steps are repealed until the victim can breathe again or until medical assistance is available.

  • Before CPR: Dial 911. With casualty lying on back, lift their chin by tilting head backwards and check breathing by listening.
  • 30 compressions: In the centre of the casualty’s chest, place hands on top of each other. Push hard and fast with body weight at a 2 inch depth to perform at 100 compressions per minute.
  • 2 Rescue Breaths: Tilt the casualty’s head and lift their chin. Pinch their nose and place your mouth over theirs to form a seal. Then, blow into the casualty’s mouth so the chest rises.

Primary Research

Stakeholders

We gathered a list of stakeholders we could potentially interview through our personal networks and online research, and we ranked them based on priority. The high-priority and medium-priority stakeholders were contactedthrough various platforms such as LinkedIn, email, and video call to share their personal experiences with CPR. During our interviews, we asked open-ended questions based on our topic maps and collected information regarding CPR training, manikins, real life emergencies, and teaching strategies.

Topic Maps

We created a series of topic maps according to the types of stakeholders we aimed to connect with. This form of visual representation provided us with a better overview of potential discussion topics and prompted us to empathize with the interviewees.

Affinity Diagrams

Although each interviewee had their own opinions regarding CPR, we were able to categorize common and relevant responses into an affinity diagram. Digital sticky notes were created to record the interviewees’ contributions, each one containing a unique idea or key point mentioned. Common topics included manikin features, feedback devices, advice to keep children engaged, and performing CPR in real life.
